The Secret to a Perfect Sourdough Crust

After two years and more failed loaves than I care to admit, I finally cracked it. The answer isn't in the recipe it's in the steam.

The oven spring that dramatic bloom your loaf takes in the first ten minutes of baking happens only when the surface of the dough stays moist enough to keep expanding. A dry oven sets the crust too early, and you get a dense, pale brick. The solution bakers have used for centuries is steam. At home, the simplest method is a Dutch oven: the lid traps the bread's own moisture, creating a micro-steam environment. Remove the lid for the final twenty minutes to brown and blister. The result? A deep mahogany crust that shatters when you cut it, with a chewy, open crumb that makes even plain butter taste like something worth writing home about.

Baker's Notes Worth Keeping

  • 01 Weigh everything. Volume measurements are inconsistent a cup of flour can vary by up to 30g depending on how it's scooped. A scale is the single highest-return piece of kit you can own.
  • 02 Egg and butter temperature matters. Cold eggs can cause batters to split; cold butter won't cream properly. Unless a recipe specifies otherwise, room temperature means it.
  • 03 Ovens lie. An oven thermometer costs almost nothing and will save you years of wondering why your cakes always come out dense. Most home ovens run 10–25°C off.
  • 04 Resting dough isn't optional. Gluten needs time to relax after mixing and kneading. Skipping rest periods is why rolled pastry shrinks back and bread is tough.
